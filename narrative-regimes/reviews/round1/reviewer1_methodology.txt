Reviewer #1 Report - Methodology / Bayesian Filtering / Portfolio Optimization
Manuscript: "Narrative Regimes: LLM-Augmented Strategic Asset Allocation for Long-Horizon Investors"
Venue: Decision Support Systems

SUMMARY OF CONTRIBUTIONS
The paper proposes an online Bayesian regime filter that fuses a rolling Gaussian return likelihood with an LLM-style narrative likelihood, coupled to a "regime-policy-mix" allocator that averages per-regime mean-variance optima by the posterior and is claimed to approximate a Markov-modulated CRRA policy. Evaluation is conducted on a Monte-Carlo synthetic world with configurable SNR, aiming to eliminate look-ahead bias by construction and to derive an adoption threshold for LLM signals. The decision-support framing (SNR threshold as an objective adoption criterion) is the most novel element.

MAJOR COMMENTS

1. Claim of CRRA-optimality of the regime-policy-mix allocator is overstated. Section 3.3 (and allocator.py lines 88-135) computes per-regime mean-variance optima and averages them by the posterior: w_t = sum_k p_k w_k*(gamma). The paper claims this "approximates the Markov-modulated CRRA policy of Guidolin & Timmermann (2007) at first order." This is not correct in general. The exact one-period CRRA/MV optimum under regime uncertainty is w* = argmax sum_k p_k (mu_k' w - gamma/2 w' Sigma_k w), which gives w* = (sum_k p_k Sigma_k)^{-1} (sum_k p_k mu_k)/gamma -- NOT a posterior average of per-regime optima (Jensen's inequality; argmax does not commute with convex combination). True Markov-modulated CRRA (Ang & Bekaert 2002; Guidolin & Timmermann 2007) additionally requires a continuation value / value-function iteration over P, which is entirely absent here. The allocator is a defensible heuristic, but the theoretical label must be downgraded. Please either (a) prove the first-order claim precisely (with error bounds in terms of regime dispersion), (b) compare against the exact mixed-moment MV (already implemented as 'moment_mix' in allocator.py line 114) and the Bellman-iterated policy, or (c) rename the method as a heuristic and drop the "CRRA-preserving" language.

2. Narrative likelihood is hard-coded to the simulator's data-generating process. In regime_filter.py lines 118-123 and the simulator (simulator.py lines 150-173), both the signal and the filter use identical mu_k = e_k with isotropic Gaussian noise. This makes the SNR sensitivity experiment a filter-calibration exercise against a known DGP rather than a genuine test of robustness to LLM-signal misspecification. Reported results thus likely overstate the value of narrative augmentation. Please add (i) a mis-specified likelihood experiment (e.g., biased mean vectors, heteroskedastic noise, correlated signal errors), and (ii) an experiment in which the filter does not know beta and must estimate it or uses a diffuse prior on sigma^2.

3. Transition matrix update is not a proper Bayesian estimator. Lines 142-145 of regime_filter.py define P <- (1-lambda) P + lambda * normalized outer product of prev_post and post. This is an ad-hoc exponential-moving-average of a rank-1 co-occurrence matrix -- not the standard Baum-Welch / forward-backward EM update, nor a Dirichlet-posterior soft-count update. Two problems follow: (a) because "prev_post" is the filtered (not smoothed) posterior, the estimator is biased toward the current observation and systematically underestimates off-diagonal transitions at slow mixing; (b) with lambda=0.05 from a diffuse prior P = 0.8 I + 0.2/K 11', the estimator is unlikely to have converged within T=360 steps. The paper should either justify this form as a stochastic-approximation EM step (with a reference) or replace it with a Dirichlet soft-count update and report sensitivity of results to the choice.

4. Soft-assignment Welford recursion lacks an effective-sample-size safeguard and weight-normalization. Lines 132-140 accumulate sum_w, sum_wx, sum_wxx using raw posteriors and estimate mu_k, cov_k by direct division. Because posteriors can concentrate near zero for the stress regime for long periods, the per-regime ESS can become extremely small and cov_k estimates will be dominated by a handful of high-weight observations. A small ridge (1e-6 I) is added but no down-weighting or prior regularization is used. Typical practice (Cappe, Moulines, Ryden 2005) introduces an informative inverse-Wishart prior or at least caps cov updates by ESS. This matters because CRRA-CE is sensitive to Sigma in the stress regime (gamma=5, extreme returns). Please add an ESS diagnostic, ideally per-regime, and show CRRA-CE as a function of an informative-prior strength.

5. CRRA certainty equivalent computation appears to double-annualize. In backtest.py lines 91-97 the CE is computed on monthly portfolio returns as u = ((1+pr)^(1-gamma) - 1)/(1-gamma), ce = (1 + (1-gamma) mean(u))^{1/(1-gamma)} - 1, and then line 107 reports crra_ce_annual = (1+ce)^12 - 1. Strictly, the CE of a stream of monthly returns should be computed as the fixed monthly return that equates mean monthly utility, then compounded by 12; but the transformation ce_monthly -> (1+ce_monthly)^12 - 1 is only exact when the investor has log utility (gamma=1). For gamma=5 with non-log utility over terminal wealth, this is an approximation that can bias the CRRA-CE upward or downward. At minimum, the paper should state the approximation explicitly and report the exact time-aggregated CE computed via the product (1+pr_t) over the horizon. Also note that the moment-mix baseline is constructed in Section 3.3 and quoted qualitatively in the Discussion but is NOT reported in Table 1 or elsewhere -- this is the single most natural ablation and its omission is conspicuous.

6. Look-ahead audit is partial. backtest.py line 36 correctly lags target weights by one period. However, the filter (regime_filter.py lines 111-114) snapshots (mu_k, cov_k, P) BEFORE updating with t's observation, then computes log-likelihood for the return observed at time t (line 108, 116). This is the intended "filtered" posterior conditional on r_t, which is fine -- but then the allocator.py consumes mu_k_series[t] / cov_k_series[t] (the pre-update snapshot) while the weights are applied to r_{t+1} via the shift(1) in backtest.py. Net effect: the investor at date t uses parameters estimated through t-1 and a return r_t they have not yet observed -- OK for weights applied at t+1. This is correct but subtle; please add a figure or paragraph explicitly labeling the information set at each step. More importantly, simulator.py draws all T returns in one pass with a single seed before the filter runs, which is fine statistically but means regime realizations are coupled across strategies on a given seed -- please confirm that Monte-Carlo averages in Tables 1-3 are computed on common random numbers (the paper implies yes; code does not explicitly enforce it).

MINOR COMMENTS

a) The comparison to Ang & Bekaert (2002) and Campbell-Viceira is invoked in the intro but never operationalized. Neither an approximate-analytic CV policy nor an explicit long-horizon (multi-period) Bellman baseline is run. Add at least one such comparison even on a reduced horizon.

b) The narrative signal uses std 1/snr (simulator.py line 171), while the paper defines sigma^2 = 1/beta. These differ by a square root; the filter uses -0.5 * beta * ||s - e_k||^2 which corresponds to sigma^2 = 1/beta. The simulator therefore generates noise with std 1/beta, not 1/sqrt(beta). This is a misalignment between text and code and changes the numeric interpretation of "SNR = 1" (in the code, SNR=1 means std=1 per coordinate, while the filter treats precision = 1). Please reconcile.

c) Stationary-distribution prior (regime_filter.py lines 96-98) is computed from the hard-coded initial P, not re-initialized when P is updated. Since P evolves, the initial prior is only used once; this is fine but deserves a sentence.

d) allocator.py line 125-127 imports copy inside the loop; minor style issue but worth cleaning.

e) Sample sizes are small: 30 paths for Table 1, 20 for Table 2, 12 for Table 3. Report standard errors and ideally paired tests (since paths are CRN). The CRRA-CE difference 0.0236 vs 0.0256 (risk parity) is well within a plausible MC error band and the paper's claim of equivalence at "comparable turnover" is quantitatively fair but statistically unverified.

RECOMMENDATION: MAJOR REVISION

JUSTIFICATION
The paper addresses a timely problem, the decision-support framing is genuinely useful, the implementation is clean and readable, and the look-ahead-by-construction methodology is a real contribution. However, three issues prevent acceptance in the current form: (i) the central theoretical claim that the regime-policy-mix allocator approximates a Markov-modulated CRRA policy is not supported by the math and must be either proved or reframed; (ii) the narrative-likelihood experiment is nearly self-fulfilling because the filter and simulator share the same parametric form, so SNR sensitivity must be re-run under mis-specification before the SNR threshold can be trusted; (iii) the transition-matrix update and the CRRA-CE annualization each contain non-standard choices that need justification or correction. None of these is fatal -- the codebase already contains a 'moment_mix' baseline, soft-count estimators are a one-function replacement, and the CE formula is a small fix -- but each materially affects headline claims and Table 1. I would be enthusiastic about a revised version that tightens the theory, adds the two ablations (moment-mix, mis-specified narrative), and reports MC standard errors.
